# ADR-004: Adopt FIFO Costing with Inventory Lots

**Status:** Accepted

**Date:** 2026-07-02

---

# Context

WBOS is designed for a wholesale import business.

Products are purchased in shipments from suppliers.

Each shipment may differ in:

* Purchase cost
* Currency
* Exchange rate
* Shipping cost
* Arrival date
* Supplier
* Container
* Expiry date (future)

A decision is required regarding how inventory should be valued and consumed.

Several inventory costing methods were considered.

---

# Decision

WBOS will use **FIFO (First In, First Out)** as its inventory costing methodology.

Inventory received together will be stored as an **Inventory Lot**.

Sales will consume inventory from the oldest available Lot first.

Inventory Lots become the foundation for:

* Inventory valuation
* Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
* Profit reporting
* Inventory traceability
* Future expiry tracking

---

# Philosophy

Products describe **what** we sell.

Inventory Lots describe **which inventory** we own.

Two shipments of the same Product are **not** identical inventory.

Each shipment has its own business history.

WBOS preserves that history.

---

# Example

Shipment A

```text id="6m2qwu"
Doritos

500 Units

0.180 KWD / Unit

Arrival: 2026-01-05
```

Shipment B

```text id="gl91c7"
Doritos

600 Units

0.225 KWD / Unit

Arrival: 2026-02-18
```

Both are Doritos.

They are **different Inventory Lots**.

Selling inventory consumes Shipment A before Shipment B.

---

# Why FIFO?

FIFO closely reflects how wholesale inventory is typically managed.

Advantages include:

* Realistic inventory valuation
* Accurate profit calculation
* Better stock rotation
* Natural support for expiry tracking
* Simpler warehouse operations

For imported products, FIFO closely matches how inventory physically moves through the warehouse.

---

# Inventory Lots

An Inventory Lot represents inventory received together under the same purchasing conditions.

Each Lot belongs to:

* Organization
* Product
* Supplier
* Purchase Order
* Shipment
* Warehouse

Each Lot stores:

* Original Quantity
* Remaining Quantity
* Unit Cost
* Currency
* Exchange Rate
* Arrival Date

Future fields may include:

* Batch Number
* Manufacturing Date
* Expiry Date
* Container Number
* Customs Reference

---

# Inventory Consumption

Inventory is consumed from the oldest available Lot.

Example:

```text id="7ludmb"
Lot A

Remaining: 120

↓

Lot B

Remaining: 250

↓

Lot C

Remaining: 400
```

Selling 150 units results in:

```text id="x3e0m2"
Lot A

120 Used

↓

Lot B

30 Used

↓

Lot C

Untouched
```

The cost of the sale reflects the Lots that were consumed.

---

# Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

COGS is calculated from the Lots consumed by a sale.

This ensures that:

* Profit reflects actual purchasing costs.
* Historical profitability remains accurate.
* Reports remain trustworthy.

COGS should never depend on current product cost.

It depends on historical inventory.

---

# Inventory Valuation

Current inventory value is derived from remaining quantities in Inventory Lots.

Conceptually:

```text id="u2mjlwm"
Remaining Quantity

×

Unit Cost

=

Lot Value
```

Summing all remaining Lot values produces the total inventory valuation.

---

# Traceability

Inventory Lots provide complete traceability.

WBOS can answer questions such as:

* Which shipment supplied this product?
* Which supplier sold it to us?
* What did it cost?
* When did it arrive?
* Which invoices consumed it?

This level of traceability would not be possible if inventory were stored as a single quantity.

---

# Relationship to the Inventory Ledger

Inventory Lots and Inventory Transactions work together.

Inventory Lots represent inventory ownership.

Inventory Transactions represent inventory movement.

Example:

```text id="6tw5i5"
Purchase Receipt

↓

Inventory Lot Created

↓

Inventory Transaction Created

↓

Sale

↓

Inventory Transaction

↓

Inventory Lot Quantity Reduced
```

The ledger records **what happened**.

The Lot records **what inventory remains**.

---

# Currency

Inventory Lots preserve the original purchasing currency.

Examples:

* KWD
* USD
* EUR

Each Lot stores:

* Original Currency
* Original Unit Cost
* Exchange Rate
* Base Currency Cost

Historical purchasing costs never change.

Future exchange rate updates must not modify historical Lots.

---

# Why Not Average Cost?

Weighted Average Cost was considered.

It was rejected.

Reasons include:

* Reduces traceability.
* Makes historical cost analysis more difficult.
* Does not reflect physical inventory movement.
* Makes expiry management harder.
* Makes shipment-level profitability less accurate.

Average costing is simpler but loses valuable business information.

---

# Why Not Last Cost?

Using only the latest purchase price was rejected.

It produces misleading profit calculations.

Example:

Products sold today may have been purchased months ago at different costs.

Historical profit should always reflect historical purchasing.

---

# Why Inventory Lots Instead of Product Cost?

Products represent catalog information.

Inventory Lots represent physical inventory.

Separating these concepts allows:

* Multiple purchasing costs
* Historical valuation
* Supplier analysis
* Shipment analysis
* Inventory aging
* FIFO

A single Product cost cannot accurately represent imported inventory over time.

---

# Future Capabilities

Inventory Lots naturally support:

* Expiry management
* Batch recalls
* Warehouse transfers
* Inventory aging reports
* Supplier quality analysis
* Customs tracking
* Barcode receiving
* AI purchasing recommendations

The architecture should not require redesign as these capabilities are introduced.

---

# Trade-Offs

FIFO introduces additional complexity.

Examples include:

* Lot allocation during sales
* Lot management
* Remaining quantity tracking
* COGS calculation

These trade-offs are acceptable because they significantly improve reporting accuracy and business insight.

---

# Architectural Rules

The following rules apply throughout WBOS.

* Every stock receipt creates an Inventory Lot.
* Inventory Lots are never merged.
* Historical Lot costs never change.
* Sales consume the oldest available Lot.
* Inventory valuation is derived from Lots.
* COGS is derived from consumed Lots.
* Products never own inventory cost.

---

# Alternatives Considered

## Weighted Average Cost

Rejected.

Simpler implementation, but reduced traceability and less accurate historical reporting.

---

## Last Purchase Cost

Rejected.

Poor representation of historical profitability.

---

## Product-Level Cost

Rejected.

Unable to represent multiple purchasing costs for the same Product.

---

# Consequences

Inventory becomes significantly more valuable than a simple stock counter.

WBOS gains:

* Accurate profitability
* Reliable inventory valuation
* Shipment-level traceability
* Better reporting
* Support for future warehouse features

The additional complexity is localized within the inventory domain.

Other domains continue to work with Products rather than Inventory Lots.

---

# Review Criteria

This decision should be reconsidered only if:

* The business fundamentally changes its inventory practices.
* Regulatory requirements require another valuation method.
* A future deployment targets industries where FIFO is inappropriate.

For wholesale food imports, FIFO remains the preferred approach.

---

# Final Decision

WBOS will use FIFO costing backed by Inventory Lots.

This approach best reflects the operational reality of our business while providing accurate inventory valuation, trustworthy profitability, and complete inventory traceability.

Products describe **what** we sell.

Inventory Lots preserve **how we acquired it**.

That distinction is fundamental to the design of WBOS.
