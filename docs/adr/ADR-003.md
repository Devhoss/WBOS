# ADR-003: Adopt an Immutable Transaction-Based Inventory Ledger

**Status:** Accepted

**Date:** 2026-07-02

---

# Context

Inventory is the operational heart of WBOS.

Every purchasing workflow, warehouse operation, sale, return, adjustment, report, and profit calculation depends on inventory accuracy.

A decision was required regarding how inventory should be represented.

Several approaches were considered:

* Store the current stock quantity directly on each Product.
* Update inventory values in place whenever stock changes.
* Use an immutable transaction ledger.
* Implement full Event Sourcing with CQRS.

This ADR explains why WBOS adopts an immutable inventory ledger.

---

# Decision

WBOS will implement inventory using an **immutable transaction ledger**.

Inventory is **never edited directly**.

Instead, every inventory movement creates a permanent Inventory Transaction.

Current stock is calculated from those transactions.

Inventory history becomes the source of truth.

---

# Philosophy

Inventory is not a number.

Inventory is the result of everything that has happened.

The quantity shown on screen is only a snapshot of a much larger history.

WBOS stores the history.

The current quantity is derived from that history.

---

# Example

Instead of storing:

```text
Doritos

Current Stock = 482
```

WBOS stores:

```text
+500  Initial Receipt

-20   Customer Sale

-3    Damaged

+5    Customer Return
```

Current Stock:

```text
500

-20

-3

+5

= 482
```

The ledger tells the complete story.

---

# Why We Chose This Approach

The transaction ledger provides several important benefits.

---

# Complete Audit History

Every inventory movement is permanently recorded.

The system can always answer:

* Why did stock change?
* Who changed it?
* When did it change?
* Which workflow caused it?
* Which document created it?

No information is lost.

---

# Trust

Business users eventually trust software only when they trust the numbers.

The ledger allows every inventory quantity to be explained.

Nothing is hidden.

Nothing is silently overwritten.

---

# Simplicity

Although transaction-based inventory sounds advanced, it is actually simpler than maintaining editable stock quantities.

The rule becomes:

Every inventory movement creates one Inventory Transaction.

No exceptions.

---

# Reporting

Because every movement is preserved, reporting becomes straightforward.

Examples include:

* Daily stock movement
* Inventory history
* Product movement
* Warehouse activity
* Inventory valuation
* Sales analysis

No additional tracking tables are required.

---

# Future Features

The ledger naturally supports:

* FIFO costing
* Inventory Lots
* Warehouse transfers
* Physical inventory counts
* Returns
* Damage tracking
* Expiry management
* Business analytics

Future features build on the same foundation.

---

# Inventory Transactions

Each Inventory Transaction represents one completed business event.

Examples include:

* Initial Stock
* Purchase Receipt
* Customer Sale
* Customer Return
* Supplier Return
* Warehouse Transfer
* Stock Adjustment
* Write-Off
* Physical Count Adjustment

Every inventory change belongs to one transaction type.

---

# Immutability

Inventory Transactions are immutable.

They represent historical facts.

Historical facts should not change.

If a mistake occurs:

Do not edit the transaction.

Create a correcting transaction.

History remains truthful.

---

# Corrections

Example:

Incorrect Receipt

```text
+100
```

Correct value:

```text
+90
```

Do not modify the original transaction.

Instead:

```text
+100 Original Receipt

-10 Correction
```

The ledger remains complete.

---

# Relationship to Business Workflows

Inventory Transactions are not created manually.

They are created automatically by workflows.

Examples:

Receive Shipment

↓

Inventory Receipt Transaction

Create Invoice

↓

Sale Transaction

Customer Return

↓

Return Transaction

Warehouse Transfer

↓

Transfer Transaction

Users complete business workflows.

WBOS creates inventory history.

---

# Current Stock

Current stock is derived.

It is never manually maintained.

Conceptually:

```text
Current Stock

=

Sum(All Inventory Transactions)
```

For performance reasons, WBOS may later maintain cached inventory summaries.

However, those summaries are considered **derived data**.

The ledger always remains the source of truth.

---

# Relationship to Inventory Lots

Inventory Lots represent inventory batches.

Inventory Transactions represent inventory movement.

The two concepts are complementary.

Example:

Shipment

↓

Inventory Lot

↓

Inventory Transactions consume quantities from Lots using FIFO.

---

# Why Not Store Stock on Products?

Example:

```text
Product

Stock = 482
```

This appears simple.

However it introduces problems.

Questions become difficult to answer.

Example:

Why is stock 482?

Who changed it?

What changed yesterday?

Which shipment supplied this inventory?

Which invoice reduced stock?

How do we recover after mistakes?

The stock quantity contains no history.

History must be recreated from logs, if it exists at all.

---

# Why Not Edit Transactions?

Historical facts should never change.

Editing transactions destroys auditability.

It becomes impossible to know:

* What was originally recorded?
* Who corrected it?
* Why it changed?

Corrections preserve truth.

Editing destroys it.

---

# Why Not Full Event Sourcing?

Full Event Sourcing was considered.

It was intentionally rejected.

Reasons include:

* Increased architectural complexity.
* Separate read models.
* Event replay infrastructure.
* Event versioning.
* Event buses.
* Higher development cost.

WBOS does not currently require these capabilities.

An immutable transaction ledger provides nearly all of the business benefits with significantly less complexity.

Should future requirements justify Event Sourcing, the transaction ledger provides an excellent foundation.

---

# Trade-Offs

The chosen approach introduces additional implementation work.

Examples include:

* Stock calculation
* Ledger queries
* Transaction validation
* Correction workflows

These costs are acceptable because they improve:

* Reliability
* Auditability
* Reporting
* Trust

---

# Architectural Rules

The following rules are mandatory.

* Inventory quantities are never edited directly.
* Every inventory movement creates one Inventory Transaction.
* Transactions are immutable.
* Corrections create additional transactions.
* Inventory history is permanent.
* Current stock is derived.
* Business workflows create transactions automatically.
* Users should never manipulate the ledger directly.

---

# Future Evolution

The ledger naturally supports future capabilities including:

* FIFO costing
* Inventory Lots
* Expiry tracking
* Warehouse transfers
* Barcode scanning
* Mobile warehouse workflows
* Demand forecasting
* AI inventory analysis
* Inventory valuation
* Historical reporting

No redesign should be required.

---

# Alternatives Considered

## Editable Stock Quantity

Rejected.

Although simple initially, it provides poor auditability and limited reporting.

---

## Hybrid Model

Rejected.

Maintaining editable stock alongside transaction history introduces synchronization risks.

One source of truth is preferred.

---

## Full Event Sourcing + CQRS

Rejected for Phase 1.

The additional infrastructure is not justified by the current business requirements.

The chosen ledger captures the majority of the business value while remaining significantly simpler.

---

# Consequences

Inventory becomes one of the strongest and most reliable parts of WBOS.

Business users gain confidence because every stock quantity can be explained.

Developers benefit from a consistent model that supports reporting, auditing, costing, and future expansion.

The inventory engine becomes a long-term foundation rather than a short-term implementation.

---

# Review Criteria

This decision should only be reconsidered if:

* Inventory requirements fundamentally change.
* Business workflows can no longer be represented as transactions.
* Event Sourcing provides measurable business value beyond the existing ledger.

At present, none of these conditions apply.

---

# Final Decision

WBOS will implement inventory using an immutable transaction ledger.

We intentionally choose this approach because it provides the strongest balance of:

* Simplicity
* Auditability
* Reliability
* Maintainability
* Future extensibility

The ledger is not merely a technical implementation.

It is a representation of how the business operates.

Inventory is not a number.

**Inventory is history.**
